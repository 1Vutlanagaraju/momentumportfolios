Why Momentum Works (Until It Doesn’t) – Myths & Facts

Momentum investing has long fascinated both academics and investors because of its seemingly simple premise: buy what’s going up and sell what’s going down. Decades of global market data show that momentum strategies often outperform traditional benchmarks over time. The reason lies in investor behavior—trends persist because people underreact to new information and are slow to adjust their expectations, allowing momentum to capture these gradual shifts in sentiment.

But momentum doesn’t always work, and that’s where myths can confuse investors. Some believe momentum is just “chasing past performance,” when in reality it’s grounded in empirical evidence across multiple markets. Another common myth is that momentum only works in bull runs. In fact, well-constructed momentum portfolios can thrive in different conditions—though they may experience sharp drawdowns during sudden market reversals, when yesterday’s winners quickly become today’s losers.

The key to using momentum wisely is discipline and diversification. Investors should understand that short-term underperformance is part of the strategy’s nature, not a failure of the concept itself. By combining momentum with risk controls, position sizing, and complementary factors like value or quality, investors can reduce volatility and improve long-term results. In other words, momentum works because human behavior doesn’t change—but it doesn’t work all the time, and acknowledging that truth is what separates successful practitioners from frustrated trend-chasers.
